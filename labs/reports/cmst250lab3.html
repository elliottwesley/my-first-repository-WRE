Objectives
This lab, centered around the physical topology of computer networks, was made up of four parts.  
Objective 1 of 4: Assemble a straight-through network cable.
The first and most time-consuming part of the lab involved transformation of a Category 5 (CAT5) unshielded twisted pair (UTP) cable into a straight-through cable, used to connect a computer to a network.  
Objective 2 of 4: Research cable types other than UTP.
The focus of the second part of the lab was research of various types of network cable other than UTP.  Some lengths of coaxial cable and of fiber-optic cable were provided to be examined
Objective 3 of 4: Wire a patch panel.
The third objective was to use a punch-down tool to connect wiring to a patch panel.
Objective 4 of 4: The data (wall) jack.
Lastly was an explanation of how a data jack could be attached to a patch panel, also using the punch-down tool.

  Notes, Observations, and Questions
Objective 1 of 4
Creating a network connection cable out of CAT5 requires a great deal of practice and patience.  After stripping three-fourths inches of insulation off of an end of cable, I attempted to untangle and straighten each individual cable before arranging them in a proper order dictated by the T568B wiring standard [Fig. 1].  This is the most difficult part of the process of creating such a cable, as one needs a little bit of strength to get the kinks out of each individual cable and to hold them in place when arranging them in the correct sequence.  The last step before inserting the wires into a connector and crimping is to cut them to an equivalent length about half an inch from the edge of the insulated cover. This was the step where I had the most problems, as I cut all eight wires too short the first time and wires 7 and 8 short the second time.  The cable tester’s flashing numbers in both instances reflected this [Fig. 2]. Had I successfully assembled the cable, I would have substituted it in place of a similar functioning cable in the laboratory to confirm its ability to work.
Objective 2 of 4
Several lengths of coaxial (coax) cable and fiber optic cable were provided for the purpose of examining them, but to help explain these types for this journal, I located an online publication from the Florida Center for Instructional Technology (2013) that provides concrete details on these different materials.  In the chapter “Cabling”, the guide describes coax as a copper conductor sheathed by a layer of plastic and a layer of braided metal (Coaxial Cable, para. 1). Coax comes in two main varieties: thin coax or Thinnet, which is ideal for linear bus networks (Coaxial Cable, para. 3); and thick coax or Thicknet, which has an additional plastic layer for waterproofing and is good for even longer bus networks, but is a lot more rigid in structure (Coaxial Cable, para. 4).  Fiber optic cables comprise a protected glass core and transmits network data via light rather than electricity (Fiber Optic Cable, para. 1). It can send data over greater distances and at greater speeds than coaxial or UTP cable, but is harder to install (Fiber Optic Cable, para. 2).
Objective 3 of 4
I was presented with a patch panel (Fig. 3) with a UTP cable stripped and its eight individual wires placed.  All I had to do was use the punch-down tool, a spring-loaded gadget functionally similar to a center punch, to affix the wires.  The tool conveniently cut off any excess material.
Objective 4 of 4
No one got to this point in the laboratory before the class period ended, but it would have involved an activity similar to the previous.  A wall jack would be added to the wiring assembly using the punch-down tool.
