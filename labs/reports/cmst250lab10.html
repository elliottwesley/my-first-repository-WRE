Notes, Observations, and Questions
Objective 1 of 2: Construct and program a functional network.
This part is more or less exactly what we did in Lab 8 - Subnetting, minus dividing the network into subnetworks.  See Figure 1 for the schematic and Figure 2.
Sometime before this lab I decided to reload the RPi with UbuntuMATE 16.04 (a.k.a. Xenial Xerus) in place of Raspbian.  This would prove to be a liability when assigning static IP address, as UbuntuMATE (and perhaps Ubuntu and other derivatives) requires a different procedure to manually assign an IP address.  Rather than using SSH or the terminal to access a dhcpcd.conf file, a “network-manager” application is necessary (Figure 3).
Objective 2 of 2: Find and fix an error in the network.
After we assembled our working network, we were asked to leave the room while the Professor deliberately sabotaged our creation to cause it to not work.
We returned with only ten minutes remaining in the class, and a piece of advice: we wouldn’t need to re-assign IP addresses to any part of the network.  This of course makes sense: a mass reconfiguration would be a waste of time considering that most errors in a network are caused by deterioration of cabling and media.
Attempts to ping the other subnetworks and even the nearest router via the computers failed.  The routers could only ping themselves; they could not ping any of the computers in the network.  If the Professor did not reprogram the routers (which he didn’t, closer examination confirmed this), then he must have tampered with the hardware.  The wires didn’t appear to be tampered with or cut on the surface, so what could he have done? We discovered the answer to this by examining the cables beneath the desks in the laboratory: the Professor had disconnected one end of a cable connecting the switch to one of the computers and replaced the open end with a separate cable as a decoy.  Sneaky!

Conclusions
In this lab, I learned about what I.T. workers do to maintain a network in a way that is both timely and economical.  The majority of fixes a network would need are related to wear and tear of media and devices--rarely if at all deliberate sabotage, like in this exercise--that can be rectified by pinging to find the exact locations where a cable, switch, or router might need to be replaced.  Once found, there is only the matter of replacing that particular medium or device--no wasted time reprogramming static IP addresses or similar when it's not necessary.
